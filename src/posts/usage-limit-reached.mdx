Bumping into [Context length exceeded](https://platform.openai.com/docs/api-reference/runs) and [Token budget exhausted](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/prompt-library/library) feels like bumping into GitHub or StackOverFlow system down, or worse yet, like network down or VPN down. I remember the feeling of *"Right then, time to go home."*, that feeling both of nuisance and of relief, invariably superseded by the latter, once the vast majority of focus always is allocated to automation, the plumbing and the testing, rather than to the actual product. There was literally nothing you could do to carry on coding, and that's when you'd notice the unpixeled world around you, and that there is indeed life outside of a computer screen.

There is however a stark contrast between a lack of tokens and a lack of computer network. And a stark contrast between how different seniorities in engineering react to a lack of tokens, and an important phenomena that the difference signals. If you matured your seniority in the Pre-GitHub Copilot era, you were raised in the era of underlined textbooks, Google Search, waiting for that one StackOverFlow question to annoint knighthood to that one answer with the honourable **Accepted Answer** ribbon, waiting for that GitHub issue to be granted citizenship to the illustrious society of its Closed Issues, waiting for that one brilliant colleague that could mythically fix everything and anything, whether or not they had encountered it before or otherwise, whose brain would somehow saved every memory and every slack exchange as an invaluable piece of family crockery, passed on from neuron to neuron, safely through the ages of its aging brain tissue, to assist precisely you in that very moment of hardship, which you will remember forever for the fellowship it demonstrated more than the value it produced, which will forever motivate you to become that very same brilliant engineer in someone else's life. Engineers matured in this era are the dinossaurs of the industry, and the metaphor applies both to the time scale and to their extinction. The vast majority of the source code of my skill had its Long Term Support revoked in the GitHub Copilot era: the era of AI enhanced engineering.

Engineers exiting their CS degrees into the industry soon before the GitHub Copilot era have about the same sense of nostalgia towards the old era, that I had towards writing a socket in C when I then learnt I could simply import it as a module in Java. This was transformational for me in my undergrads, in ways I couldn't then comprehend: When I discovered the socket I had to implement in C for Distributed Systems could simply be imported in Java in Cyber Security. I knew it then there was something valuable about that painfully learnt lesson, but I'd much rather secretly despise it. It made me feel cooler somehow. When you're an undergrad, opinions are sexier than wisdom.

I believe compiler developers would look at me then the same way I look at junior engineers now: With a sense of responsibility to finding a common language to tell me of the low level world, not for the sake of the knowledge of its logical laws, but for the sake of the wisdom produced by having to navigate it, and for the sake of the fellowship produced by having to endure it.

Using assistants for everything and anything rewards cognitive economy, it reinforces in your brain behind the scenes, a bias towards not only coding, but towards learning and understanding in general. It delivers you into this flow of creation, where anything and anything is possible, where the only limits to your creativity are creativity itself, natural needs like water, food and sleep, a healthy session context and token limit. Provided you know what to ask of these assistants, and how to ask it, your brain won't reward anything else other than these two new skills.

So now we're back to the original point: What happens when you bump into the token limit? Do you go home? Or do you still have it in you, the pre-historic curiosity or understanding of the world, the pre-historic joy of learning, searching, moving slowly through trial and error?
