The AI Engineering (AIE) 2026 conference in London made one thing clear: while artificial intelligence is significantly changing how we build software, the role of the software engineer is far from obsolete. Instead, the profession is moving towards a higher level of abstraction, where development can happen at the speed of thought. That shift, however, brings with it an entirely new set of engineering challenges.

## The Rise of Harness Engineering

As foundational models became increasingly capable of producing complex, high-quality solutions from only a handful of examples, the industry quickly learned to offload more coding work to agentic assistants. In doing so, the primary bottleneck shifted from writing code to ensuring that these agents [code reliably](https://www.louisbouchard.ai/harness-engineering/). The challenge is now to mitigate infinite loops, misalignment, context rot, and the propagation of poor architectural decisions. To address these issues, the industry has formalised **Harness Engineering**.

A useful way to frame this is through the equation *[Agent = Model + Harness](https://martinfowler.com/articles/harness-engineering.html)*. While the AI model acts as a non-deterministic token generator, the harness provides the surrounding environment and guardrails, including [state management, tool execution capabilities, observability, feedback loops, and strict mechanical constraints](https://blog.langchain.com/the-anatomy-of-an-agent-harness/). Engineers now design feedforward controls in natural language, alongside automated component evaluation and prompt fine-tuning frameworks, to steer the agent predictably before it generates code. They also build feedback controls, such as custom linters and tests, to help the agent self-correct more accurately.

## Why Software Engineers Are Not Obsolete

Although agents are increasingly capable of producing functional, high-quality code autonomously, the human role remains essential. What is being removed is not the engineer, but much of the repetitive plumbing work that has historically consumed so much of the profession's energy. What remains is the part that matters most: judgment, abstraction, system design, and orchestration.

Where software engineers once owned the software engineering cycle end to end — from domain understanding and specification refinement to infrastructure, implementation, testing, deployment, monitoring, and maintenance — that cycle is now increasingly shared between the human and the agent. The agent can work tirelessly across every part of the process that the engineer is able to formalise and automate.

This has always been one of software engineering's defining characteristics: its unmatched capacity for automation. Agents do not replace that principle; they extend it. In that sense, they bring the field closer to a longstanding ambition that began with the Universal Turing Machine and the first computer programs: the decoupling of thought from its implementation.

The human engineer is therefore becoming more fully responsible for high-level design and system orchestration, while the agent handles the operational work in service of that design. Human engineers are now required to:

* **Steer the agents:** As OpenAI's Ryan Lopopolo noted, the new paradigm is that "[humans steer](https://openai.com/index/harness-engineering/) and agents execute". When an agent fails, engineers improve the harness to prevent the same class of failure from reocurring, rather than simply reprompting the coding agent.
* **Enforce architecture and specifications:** The industry is moving rapidly towards "[post-code](https://tessl.io/devcon/)" or spec-first development. Engineers must define rigorous boundaries, design highly testable "[deep modules](https://github.com/mattpocock/skills/blob/main/improve-codebase-architecture/SKILL.md)," and preserve the overall context and coherence of the system.
* **Provide judgment and taste:** As highlighted by Gergely Orosz, what continues to matter most is what AI cannot genuinely replicate: exercising [human judgment](https://andriybuday.com/category/opinion), understanding real-world business needs, and applying lived experience. AI tools may give teams extraordinary leverage, but software fundamentals, taste, and architectural discipline remain indispensable.
